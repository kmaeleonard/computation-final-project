Conclusions and Reflections

I was unable to fully answer my research questions in this project, but I think the data I collected and analyzed under the Excel section of this website provided me with a better idea of places within the texts where I look for answers.

If I wanted to continue this project, here are some ways I imagine I could. To attempt to find publications strongly engaging with social justice, I could closely read publications that cite Jones, Moore, and Walton (2016) in the methods section, publications that use social justice in the methods section, publications that use social justice in every IMRaD section, and publications that use “social justice” in the main text more times than average. I could look for limited engagement with social justice in articles that cite Jones, Moore, and Walton (2016) in the introduction/literature review sections and that use the term social justice infrequently or never in the main text.

It also seems like it would be more beneficial for me to obtain data sets from somewhere outside of Web of Science (or in addition to Web of Science) because Web of Science doesn’t have access to important social justice publications in technical communication, like Walton, Moore, and Jones’ (2019) book titled Technical Communication After the Social Justice Turn: Building Coalitions for Action. However, it seems like collecting data outside of a database might include a lot more manual work. Using Web of Science for this project felt optimal for the limited amount of time I had especially because this project was more exploratory than a formal research project.

If I were to continue with this project, I would feel most compelled to seek out publications that engage strongly, deeply, or meaningfully with social justice rather than looking for publications that engage minimally with social justice while citing social justice scholars. My positionality as a graduate student makes me hesitant to attempt to problematize how other scholars may insufficiently engage with social justice scholarship they cite, particularly because I may be in a more vulnerable position as a graduate student compared to more experienced and tenured scholars. As a first-year PhD student, I also feel I need to gain more knowledge of the field before potentially critiquing the work of more experienced scholars.

One problem I anticipate in continuing this project is that I don’t have a good way to measure engagement with social justice. I have a difficult time envisioning a universal and not overly subjective way of measuring engagement over 100+ publications. It would likely take a lot of close reading and textual analysis

Lastly, if I were to pursue this research further, I would need to more deeply frame my project from a social justice perspective and expand on threads of “positionality, privilege, and power” (Jones, Moore, and Walton (2016), p. 220) within the research.
